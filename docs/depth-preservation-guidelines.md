# CBV Depth Preservation Guidelines

## Core Principle: Maintain Biblical Richness

The Children's Bible Version (CBV) must preserve the **depth, richness, and story-like quality** of Scripture while only simplifying truly complex words. This is not a "dumbing down" of the Bible - it's making the depth accessible to children.

## Translation Philosophy: "Story-Like Depth"

### What This Means:
- **Keep the narrative flow**: Scripture tells a story - maintain that storytelling quality
- **Preserve emotional impact**: The feelings, drama, and spiritual depth should remain
- **Maintain theological richness**: Don't remove deep concepts, just explain them accessibly
- **Keep biblical tone**: The unique voice and rhythm of each book should be preserved
- **Only change what's necessary**: Replace only words that truly block understanding

### Approved Ages 5-7 Voice: Rich Picture Language

For ages 5-7, the CBV should help a child see the verse in their mind. Short sentences still matter, but the stronger rule is faithful, sensory, story-rich clarity. A few extra simple words are welcome when they help the child picture what is happening without changing the meaning.

**Style anchor - Genesis 1:3**

God spoke into the deep darkness that covered everything. He said, "Let there be light!" And bright light began to shine over all the darkness.

Use this as the voice target for Genesis and future narrative passages:
- Concrete picture first: darkness, water, sky, land, light, living things.
- Simple drama: God speaks, his words come to pass, and the scene changes.
- Faithful explanation: clarify what the verse means, but do not add later details into earlier verses.
- Gentle wonder: make the verse feel big, good, and safe for a young child.

### Chapter Summaries and Theological Accuracy

Chapter summaries and key lessons should be vivid, but they must stay as close as possible to the Bible's own claims and wording.

- Prefer "God speaks, and his words come to pass" over "creation obeys" unless the passage itself presents creation as personally obeying.
- Preserve important biblical wording when it carries meaning, such as "the great lights of the sky" in Genesis 1 rather than the flatter "lights in the sky."
- Summaries may connect the chapter's movements, but should not import details from later chapters or explain away tensions the text keeps open.
- Use active language for God's work: God commands, makes, separates, gathers, names, blesses, and sees that his work is good.

### Ceremonial and Legal Passages

Leviticus, Numbers, and similar passages need extra care. Preserve the specific ritual action and order, but replace bare ancient words with concrete language a child can picture.

- Use "clay bowl" instead of "earthen vessel."
- Use "fresh flowing water" instead of the ritual phrase "running water."
- Use "red thread" for "scarlet" when the object is the red thread or cord.
- Use "leafy hyssop branch" so the plant is not a mystery word.
- Use "small measure of oil," "tenth-measure," "honest dry measure," or "honest liquid measure" instead of bare terms such as "log," "ephah," or "hin."
- Keep the offering type when it matters. For ages 5-7, "grain gift," "offering for sin," and "offering for guilt" are clearer than flattening everything into "a gift."
- Do not delete numbers, animals, washing, waiting days, priestly actions, or location details just because the passage is repetitive.

Legal verses must be just as child-readable as narrative verses. For example, Leviticus 5:1 should not say "public adjuration." A faithful child-readable version can say: "If someone hears a public call to tell the truth, and he saw what happened or knows about it, he must speak up. If he stays quiet, he is guilty."

## Publication Approval

Only chapters listed in `content/bible-text/approved-chapters.json` may appear in the public reader. Imported or generated chapters are drafts until a human review confirms:

- the verse meaning has not drifted from the original reference,
- both age ranges can be read by children in that range,
- the wording keeps the visual storytelling tradition where the passage allows it,
- chapter summaries and lessons are theologically precise,
- the resource chapter includes the Genesis 1-2 style support material.

## Word Substitution Guidelines

### ✅ **Words to Simplify (Truly Complex)**
- **Archaic words**: "behold", "henceforth", "verily", "thou", "thy"
- **Very abstract concepts**: "redemption", "justification", "propitiation" (for younger ages)
- **Obscure cultural terms**: "cubit", "ephod", "selah" (with explanation)
- **Complex theological terms**: "omniscient", "immutable", "transcendent" (for younger ages)

### ❌ **Words to Keep (Preserve Depth)**
- **Emotional words**: "love", "joy", "peace", "hope", "faith"
- **Action words**: "created", "spoke", "called", "made", "gave"
- **Descriptive words**: "formless", "empty", "deep", "hovering", "darkness"
- **Relational words**: "Father", "Son", "Spirit", "God", "Lord"
- **Story elements**: "earth", "waters", "light", "sky", "land"

## Age-Level Depth Preservation

### Ages 5-7: Simple Richness
**Goal**: Keep the story feel with simple words

**Example - Psalm 23:1**:
- *Original*: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want."
- *CBV 5-7*: "The Lord is my shepherd. I have everything I need."
- *Depth Kept*: Shepherd relationship, personal care, provision

**Strategy**:
- Break long sentences but keep all parts
- Replace only truly difficult words
- Maintain the emotional core
- Keep the storytelling rhythm

### Ages 8-10: Growing Richness
**Goal**: Maintain most original language with minimal changes

**Example - Psalm 23:1**:
- *Original*: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want."
- *CBV 8-10*: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want."
- *Depth Kept*: All original language, full meaning

**Strategy**:
- Keep original sentence structure
- Replace only archaic words
- Maintain theological terms
- Preserve literary quality

### Ages 11-12: Full Richness
**Goal**: Almost identical to original with minimal changes

**Example - Psalm 23:1**:
- *Original*: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want."
- *CBV 11-12*: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want."
- *Depth Kept**: Complete original meaning and language

**Strategy**:
- Change only truly necessary words
- Maintain all theological depth
- Keep literary and poetic elements
- Prepare for adult Bible transition

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Genesis 1:2
**Original**: "Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters."

**Ages 5-7**: "The earth was empty and didn't have a shape yet. There was complete darkness over the deep waters. And God's Spirit was hovering over the waters."
- **Changes**: "formless" → "didn't have a shape", added "complete" for clarity
- **Depth Kept**: All imagery, mystery, God's presence, creative anticipation

**Ages 8-10**: "The earth was formless and empty, and darkness covered the surface of the deep. The Spirit of God was hovering over the waters."
- **Changes**: Minimal, just sentence flow
- **Depth Kept**: Almost all original language and imagery

### Example 2: John 3:16
**Original**: "For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life."

**Ages 5-7**: "God loved the world so very much that He gave us his only Son Jesus. And anyone who believes in him will not die but live forever with God."
- **Changes**: "For" → removed, "whoever" → "anyone", "perish" → "die", "eternal life" → "live forever with God"
- **Depth Kept**: God's love, sacrifice, belief, salvation, eternal life

**Ages 8-10**: "God loved the world so much that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life."
- **Changes**: Minimal, just "For" removed
- **Depth Kept**: All original theological meaning

### Example 3: Psalm 23:4
**Original**: "Even though I walk through the darkest valley, I will fear no evil, for you are with me; your rod and your staff, they comfort me."

**Ages 5-7**: "Even when I walk through the darkest valley, I will not be afraid. You are with me, and your rod and staff comfort me."
- **Changes**: "fear no evil" → "not be afraid"
- **Depth Kept**: Dark valley imagery, God's presence, protection, comfort

**Ages 8-10**: "Even though I walk through the darkest valley, I will fear no evil, for you are with me; your rod and your staff, they comfort me."
- **Changes**: None needed
- **Depth Kept**: All original meaning and imagery

## Quality Control Checklist

### For Each Translation:
- [ ] **Story Flow**: Does it still read like a story?
- [ ] **Emotional Impact**: Are the feelings preserved?
- [ ] **Theological Depth**: Is the meaning intact?
- [ ] **Biblical Tone**: Does it sound like the Bible?
- [ ] **Imagery**: Are the visual elements maintained?
- [ ] **Minimal Changes**: Were only necessary words changed?

### Red Flags to Avoid:
- Removing emotional words (love, joy, peace, hope)
- Simplifying away theological concepts
- Losing the narrative flow
- Making it sound like a modern children's book
- Removing mystery or awe
- Over-explaining what should be felt

## Translation Process

### Step 1: Original Analysis
- Read the original verse multiple times
- Identify the core meaning and emotional impact
- Note the story context and flow
- Mark truly difficult words vs. accessible words

### Step 2: Age-Level Adaptation
- **Ages 5-7**: Replace only words that block understanding completely
- **Ages 8-10**: Replace only archaic or very obscure words
- **Ages 11-12**: Replace only truly necessary words

### Step 3: Depth Verification
- Read aloud - does it still sound like Scripture?
- Check emotional impact - is the feeling preserved?
- Verify theological meaning - is the depth intact?
- Test with children - do they understand and feel the impact?

### Step 4: Final Review
- Compare with original - has depth been preserved?
- Check flow - does it connect with surrounding verses?
- Verify consistency - does it match the book's tone?
- Theological review - is doctrine sound?

## Success Metrics

### Depth Preservation Indicators:
- **Children feel the emotion**: They respond to the feeling, not just the words
- **Questions show understanding**: Their questions reflect depth, not confusion
- **Memory retention**: They remember the meaning, not just simplified words
- **Spiritual impact**: The verse affects their heart and spirit
- **Biblical literacy**: They transition easily to adult Bibles

### Warning Signs:
- **Over-simplification**: Losing the biblical voice
- **Emotional flattening**: Removing the feeling
- **Theological reduction**: Losing the depth
- **Modernization**: Making it sound contemporary
- **Story loss**: Breaking the narrative flow

---

**Remember**: The goal is not to make the Bible simple - it's to make the depth of the Bible accessible to children while preserving every ounce of its richness, power, and spiritual impact.
