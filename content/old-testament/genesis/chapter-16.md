# Genesis Chapter 16

## Book Overview
Genesis 16 continues the covenant family story. Sarai gives Hagar to Abram, Hagar bears Ishmael, and the Lord sees Hagar in her suffering and speaks promises about her son.

## Important Keywords
- barren: Not able to have children
- promise: Words someone says and keeps
- angel: A messenger sent from God
- Ishmael: Abraham and Hagar's son
- mercy: Kind help given to someone in need or guilt
- spring: Water that comes up from the ground
- wilderness: A wild, lonely place away from towns
- suffering: Pain, trouble, or deep sadness
- Beer Lahai Roi: The well name connected with the God who sees

## Verse-by-Verse Translation

### Genesis 16:1
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:1 - Now Sarai, Abram's wife, bore him no children. She had a servant, an Egyptian, whose name was Hagar.

#### Ages 5-7
Abram's wife Sarai had not had any children. She had an Egyptian servant named Hagar.

#### Ages 8-10
Abram's wife Sarai had borne him no children, but she had an Egyptian servant named Hagar.

**Translation Notes**:
The verse names Hagar as Egyptian and as Sarai's servant. Keep both details because they shape the tension and help children see that Hagar is a real person in the story, not only a plot detail.

---

### Genesis 16:2
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:2 - Sarai said to Abram, "See now, Yahweh has restrained me from bearing. Please go in to my servant. It may be that I will obtain children by her." Abram listened to the voice of Sarai.

#### Ages 5-7
Sarai said to Abram, "The Lord has kept me from having children. Have a child with my servant. Maybe I can build a family through her." Abram listened to Sarai.

#### Ages 8-10
Sarai said to Abram, "The Lord has kept me from having children. Have a child with my servant; perhaps I can build a family through her." Abram listened to Sarai.

**Translation Notes**:
This verse describes Sarai's plan; it does not say God commanded the plan. Keep the family-building language, but do not make the choice sound painless or ideal.

---

### Genesis 16:3
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:3 - Sarai, Abram's wife, took Hagar the Egyptian, her servant, after Abram had lived ten years in the land of Canaan, and gave her to Abram her husband to be his wife.

#### Ages 5-7
So after Abram had lived in Canaan for ten years, Sarai gave her Egyptian servant Hagar to Abram as another wife.

#### Ages 8-10
So after Abram had lived in Canaan for ten years, Sarai gave her Egyptian servant Hagar to Abram as another wife.

**Translation Notes**:
The ten years in Canaan show waiting inside the promise story. Do not skip the number; it helps children feel the long delay behind Sarai's choice.

---

### Genesis 16:4
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:4 - He went in to Hagar, and she conceived. When she saw that she had conceived, her mistress was despised in her eyes.

#### Ages 5-7
Abram slept with Hagar, and she became pregnant. When Hagar knew she was pregnant, she looked down on Sarai.

#### Ages 8-10
Abram slept with Hagar, and she became pregnant. When Hagar knew she was pregnant, she began to despise Sarai.

**Translation Notes**:
Use plain, non-graphic language. The verse shows pregnancy followed by pride and contempt, so keep the relational break visible without adding details the verse does not give.

---

### Genesis 16:5
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:5 - Sarai said to Abram, "This wrong is your fault. I gave my servant into your bosom, and when she saw that she had conceived, she despised me. May Yahweh judge between me and you."

#### Ages 5-7
Then Sarai said to Abram, "The wrong done to me is your fault. I put my servant in your arms, and now that she is pregnant, she looks down on me. May the Lord judge between you and me."

#### Ages 8-10
Then Sarai said to Abram, "The wrong done to me is your fault. I put my servant in your arms, and now that she knows she is pregnant, she despises me. May the Lord judge between you and me."

**Translation Notes**:
Sarai's words are angry and wounded. Preserve that she appeals to the Lord as judge, but do not make every claim in her speech sound like a calm moral verdict.

---

### Genesis 16:6
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:6 - But Abram said to Sarai, "Behold, your maid is in your hand. Do to her whatever is good in your eyes." Sarai dealt harshly with her, and she fled from her face.

#### Ages 5-7
Abram said, "Your servant is in your hands. Do with her what you think is best." Then Sarai treated Hagar harshly, and Hagar ran away.

#### Ages 8-10
Abram said, "Your servant is in your hands. Do with her what you think is best." Then Sarai treated Hagar harshly, and Hagar fled from her.

**Translation Notes**:
The text says Sarai treated Hagar harshly and Hagar fled. Keep Hagar's distress clear, but avoid inventing specific actions that Genesis does not name.

### Genesis 16:8
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:8 - He said, "Hagar, Sarai's servant, where did you come from? Where are you going?" She said, "I am fleeing from the face of my mistress Sarai."

#### Ages 5-7
He said, "Hagar, servant of Sarai, where have you come from, and where are you going?" She said, "I am running away from Sarai, the woman I serve."

#### Ages 8-10
He said, "Hagar, servant of Sarai, where have you come from, and where are you going?" She said, "I am running away from my mistress Sarai."

**Translation Notes**:
The angel names Hagar and asks her story. This is an important part of the chapter's comfort: Hagar is seen, addressed, and heard.

### Genesis 16:9
**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:9 - Yahweh's angel said to her, "Return to your mistress, and submit yourself under her hands."

#### Ages 5-7
Then the angel of the Lord said to her, "Go back to the woman you serve and live under her authority."

#### Ages 8-10
Then the angel of the Lord said to her, "Go back to your mistress and submit to her authority."

**Key Vocabulary**:
- angel: A messenger sent from God

**Translation Notes**:
This command belongs to Hagar's specific story and God's promise to her. It should not be used as a general rule telling a harmed child to stay in danger; children should be protected by trustworthy adults.

**Translation Notes**:
The name Ishmael is tied to the Lord hearing Hagar's suffering. The verse gives both the child's name and the reason for it, so keep both together.

**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:12 - He will be like a wild donkey among men. His hand will be against every man, and every man's hand against him. He will live opposed to all of his brothers."

#### Ages 5-7

The wild donkey picture points to a free, hard-to-tame life marked by conflict. Do not turn it into a simple insult or remove the warning.

**Original Reference**: Genesis 16:13 - She called the name of Yahweh who spoke to her, "You are a God who sees," for she said, "Have I even stayed alive after seeing him?"

**Translation Notes**:
Hagar names the Lord from her own encounter with him. The wording should make the wonder personal: God saw Hagar in the wilderness, and she knew she had been seen.

## Chapter Summary
Sarai and Abram try to build the promised family through Hagar, but the household becomes full of pain. Hagar runs into the wilderness, and the angel of the Lord finds her by a spring. The Lord hears her suffering, promises that Ishmael's family will become too many to count, and Hagar calls him "the God who sees me."

## Key Lessons for Children
1. **God sees the lonely and hurting**: Hagar was far from the tents, but she was not hidden from the Lord.
2. **Human plans can bring real pain**: Sarai, Abram, and Hagar all live inside a hard family moment, and the chapter does not pretend it is easy.
3. **God hears suffering**: Ishmael's name reminds readers that the Lord heard Hagar's trouble.

3. What name did Hagar give to the Lord?
4. What does Ishmael's name help us remember?

### Ages 8-10
1. How did Sarai's plan create trouble in the household?
2. Why is it important that the angel called Hagar by name?
3. What promises did the Lord give about Ishmael?
4. How does Genesis 16 prepare us for the waiting in Genesis 17?
